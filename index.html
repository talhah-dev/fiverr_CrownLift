How mudjacking works

A safe, cost-effective method to lift settled concrete by filling voids beneath the slab and bringing it back to level—fast, clean, and ready to use.

1

Inspect & mark low points

We identify settlement areas, voids, and drainage concerns to plan a precise lift.

2

Drill small access holes

Small, clean holes allow controlled injection—without major disruption.

3

Pump, lift, and level

A sand-and-cement mix fills the voids and raises the slab smoothly back into position.

4

Patch & clean finish

Holes are patched, the site is cleaned, and the concrete is typically ready for use quickly.

Answers to common mudjacking questions

Quick clarity on pricing, timing, durability, and what surfaces we can lift.

Mudjacking (concrete lifting) levels sunken slabs by drilling small access holes and pumping a sand-and-cement mix underneath. The mix fills voids and gently raises the concrete back into position, then the holes are patched.

Cost depends on slab size, how far it has settled, accessibility, and how many areas need lifting. It’s commonly more cost-effective than full replacement. Request an estimate for an exact quote.

Many projects are completed in a few hours. Since there’s no long curing time like new concrete, surfaces are often usable quickly after the lift and patching.

Mudjacking uses a sand-and-cement slurry to fill voids and lift slabs. Foam lifting uses polyurethane to raise concrete with a lighter material. The best choice depends on your slab, soil conditions, and goals—an inspection helps decide.

Mudjacking is a durable repair because it fills voids beneath the slab. Long-term results also depend on drainage, soil stability, and preventing erosion—addressing root causes helps it last.

Common applications include sidewalks, driveways, patios, steps, stoops/porches, garage floors, pool decks, and many other free-floating slabs.
